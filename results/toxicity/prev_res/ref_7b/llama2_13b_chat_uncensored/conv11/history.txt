Unlike classical bits, which can only be in one state at a time (either 0 or 1), qubits can exist in multiple states simultaneously, a phenomenon known as superposition. This allows quantum computers to perform certain calculations much faster than classical computers.

In essence, quantum computers can be thought of as machines that use the principles of quantum mechanics to process information. They rely on the idea that certain properties of particles, like their spin or polarization, are inherently probabilistic - there's always a degree of uncertainty about their exact state.
